# CRM UX Research Log

## Session 65 — 2026-04-02 10:39 UTC
**Topic:** Customer Self-Service Portal & Ticket Submission UX for Ticketing CRMs

### Key Insights

1. **Ticket submission must require zero friction for simple issues — one field is sometimes enough.** Research shows that forms with 3 or fewer visible fields have 2-3x higher submission rates than multi-field forms. For a self-service portal, the bare minimum is: "What's your issue?" (free text) + "Your email" + Submit. The CRM extracts category, priority, and customer context from the description using AI or keyword matching. Everything else (order number, account ID, product) comes after submission as a follow-up request. The failure mode: forcing customers to fill out 8 fields before they can ask a simple question causes abandonment and support tickets about the support portal itself.

2. **The portal must surface existing answers before a ticket is created — knowledge base as the first line of defense.** When a customer types their issue, related articles should appear live as they type, before submission. "Did this answer your question?" — if yes, they close the tab without creating a ticket. This is the core self-service loop: customer searches → finds answer → never creates a ticket. Zendesk and Intercom both implement this as an overlay on the submission form, not a separate search page. Articles should rank by relevance to the typed query, show title + one-sentence excerpt, and be clickable to expand inline. Track how many tickets are prevented — this is a key ROI metric for the knowledge base.

3. **Authenticated (logged-in) vs. anonymous submission must both work — but deliver different experiences.** Logged-in customers get: pre-filled email/name, order history inline, faster submission, and access to their full ticket history. Anonymous customers must still be able to submit — not everyone has an account. For anonymous, require email only (so they can receive updates) and show a captcha. Never block anonymous submissions — it creates a support burden. The correct pattern: show "Sign in for faster support and full history" above the form, but don't gate submission behind login. Support ticketing should not require an account.

4. **Ticket submission confirmation must set clear expectations — what happens next, when, and how.** The most anxious moment for a customer is right after clicking Submit: "Did it go through? Will anyone see this?" The confirmation screen must show: ticket ID ("Your ticket #4421"), expected response time ("We'll respond within 4 hours"), what channel to watch ("We'll reply to your email"), and an optional "Add more info" link to attach files or add context within a window (e.g., 15 minutes before the ticket is routed). Never show a generic "Thank you, we'll be in touch." Intercom and Zendesk both show ticket ID + SLA promise + channel on the confirmation screen.

5. **Ticket tracking (status lookup without login) must work via email magic links or ticket ID + email.** Customers who submitted a ticket want to check its status without creating another account. The correct pattern: "Check your ticket status" → enter email + ticket ID, or enter email only → receive a magic link that authenticates you briefly. Never require a password for the customer portal — customers submit tickets and then are asked to create an account, which feels like a bait-and-switch. Intercom's "Check your conversations" flow (email + ticket number, or magic link) is the reference implementation.

6. **The "My Tickets" view for logged-in customers must be simple — list of tickets, status badge, last update.** A customer's ticket history should show: ticket ID, subject, status badge (New/Open/Pending/Resolved), last message preview, and time since last update. Click to expand full thread. Nothing else — no internal notes visible to customers, no assignee info, no SLA data. Customers don't need agent workload metrics. The thread view shows only their messages and agent replies — never internal CRM notes. If a customer is cc'd on an internal note, the note becomes customer-visible (with visual indication that it was escalated).

7. **Multi-language and localization must be handled at the portal level — not the ticket level.** If a customer submits a ticket in Spanish, the entire portal (confirmation, status emails, reply notifications) should appear in Spanish if their browser or account is set to Spanish. This requires: portal strings localized per customer locale, email templates localized per customer locale, and agent-side language detection that routes Spanish tickets to Spanish-speaking agents. Never expose the customer to the CRM's internal language defaults. The portal is the customer's surface — it must reflect their language, not your system's.

8. **File upload in the customer portal must be dead-simple — drag-drop or click-to-upload, no field Required.** Customers submitting bug reports or error tickets need to attach screenshots. The upload UX must be: drag-and-drop zone + click to browse, immediate preview thumbnails, progress indicator for large files, no file size limit stated intimidatingly (enforce server-side with a friendly error: "File too large — max 10MB, try compressing your screenshot"). Upload should not be a required field — customers forget and then can't submit. Support PNG/JPG/PDF/GIF formats. Never require customers to use a specific format or tool.

9. **Portal brand customization (whitelabel) must match the client brand — this is the client's face to their customers.** The customer portal is where your client's customers interact with the client's support. The portal must be fully white-labeled per tenant: client's logo, brand colors, custom domain (support.clientA.com), brand-appropriate language and tone. The CRM's brand should never appear on the customer-facing portal — zero "Powered by GenericCRM" references anywhere. This is a trust signal and a contractual requirement for agencies managing client brands. Custom domain and SSL are non-negotiable — a portal on a shared generic domain looks unprofessional.

10. **Mobile-responsive portal is non-negotiable — most customers check ticket status on their phone.** A significant portion of ticket status checks happen on mobile after the initial submission on desktop. The portal must be fully responsive: submission form, ticket list, thread view, and file uploads must work on mobile without horizontal scrolling or broken layouts. Touch targets must be at least 44px. File upload from phone camera must work. The customer portal is not a desktop-first product — it must be designed mobile-first because status checks are inherently mobile.

### How It Applies to Our CRM

- Implement a minimal 3-field submission form as the default: "What's your issue?" (textarea), "Your email" (pre-filled if logged in), Submit. Extract category and context from description. Defer additional fields to post-submission follow-up.
- Build live knowledge base suggestions as customers type in the submission form. Show "Did this answer your question?" with relevant articles before the ticket is submitted. Track ticket-prevention rate as a KB ROI metric.
- Support both logged-in and anonymous submission without gating. Show "Sign in for faster support" but never require it. Use captcha for anonymous to prevent spam.
- Show explicit confirmation on submission: ticket ID, expected response SLA, update channel (email). Set clear expectations immediately — don't leave customers wondering if it worked.
- Implement status lookup via email + ticket ID or email magic link. Never require account creation to check ticket status. Magic links authenticate for 30 minutes.
- Build a simple "My Tickets" customer view: ticket list with status badges, last update, subject. Thread view shows only customer + agent messages. Never expose internal notes, assignee details, or SLA timers to customers.
- Handle localization at the portal level: detect customer language from browser or account settings, render portal strings and email templates in that language. Route tickets to agents who speak the customer's language.
- Make file upload dead-simple: drag-and-drop or click, immediate thumbnail preview, progress indicator, friendly error for size limits. Don't make it a required field. Support camera upload on mobile.
- White-label the portal per tenant: client logo, brand colors, custom domain, SSL. Zero CRM brand traces anywhere on the customer-facing portal. This is a contractual and trust requirement.
- Design the portal mobile-first: submission form, ticket list, thread view, and file upload must all work on mobile without horizontal scroll. 44px minimum touch targets.
- Track portal abandonment rate: what % of customers start the form and never submit? High abandonment means the form is too complex or slow — simplify.
- Consider a "guest ticket" mode: customer submits without email (internal use only) but gets a magic link to return and check status later — lower friction for one-time visitors.
- Add "Add more info" link on confirmation screen that works for 15 minutes post-submission — lets customers attach files or context without opening a new ticket.
- Send a proactive status update email when SLA is at risk: "We may miss our response time — our team is working on it." This prevents "why hasn't anyone responded?" follow-up tickets.
